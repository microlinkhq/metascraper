The TSA is trying not to ruin your summer vacation

Editors, USA TODAY 4:52 p.m. EDT May 24, 2016

Passengers at Chicago O'Hare International Airport wait in line to be screened at a Transportation Security Administration checkpoint on May 16, 2016 in Chicago, Illinois.(Photo: (Photo: Scott Olson, Getty Images))

Heads are rolling at the TSA

U.S. travelers and lawmakers are freaking out about lines at airport security, and rightly so. But the TSA is trying: Head of security Kelly Hoggan was ousted this week amid the uproar over the long lines. (Also not in his favor: Intense scrutiny of $90,000 in bonus payments, which he racked up as screeners let guns slip past security.) Back to the lines. We've heard the horror stories about rolling out the cots at O'Hare. The looming crush of summer travel doesn't have us feeling overly optimistic, but Congress did approve a TSA request to hire 768 more officers by June 15 and pay more overtime. Hearings will take place Wednesday and Thursday on the issue.

While we wait for the TSA to fix the problem, (please! fix this!) here are some strategies to help you make your flight:

  • Get to the airport super early, at least two hours before boarding for domestic flights, three to four hours for international.
  • Sign up (now!) for TSA PreCheck or Global Entry, or buy priority access to TSA lines. Delta, JetBlue and United offer this.
  • Fly from less busy airports. If you live on Long Island, fly from Islip rather than from JFK, for example. Long Beach usually has shorter lines than LAX, and so on.
  • Try to fly on a Tuesday or Wednesday, when airports are less busy.
  • If all else fails, bring some joy to your fellow travelers. Tap the person next to you on the shoulder and show this video. A belly laugh can go a long way in your traveling zen.

Fill in the blank: Democracy is ___________

It's MESSY, OK? So says Bernie Sanders, who is in a bit of a mess himself after making comments that seemed to predict the Democratic convention in Philadelphia this summer would be "messy." On Tuesday, he tried to clarify. "The media often takes words out of context. The context of that is democracy is messy, that people will have a vigorous debate on the issues," the Vermont senator said. Our friends at For the Record explain what's wrong with both parties and why really, there's no one to vote for. If you're interested in Tuesday's GOP primary in Washington state, we'll have the (Trumpy) results here.

Twitter's loosening the reins but still keeping them relatively short

Rumors have been flying for months about changes to tweet lengths, and Twitter has now spoken (relatively pithily, too). Photos, videos, GIFs, polls will not count against your allotted 140 characters, though links will still take up space. Tagging O.P.H. thats other peoples handles also wont eat into your character count, so theres more room to get your message out without the ability to write an epic tome (were thankful for that). And the ultimate in narcissism: You can quote-tweet yourself, without it counting against you. Well, as far as characters go. It might count against you in other ways.

Bill Cosby is going to trial this summer

Bill Cosby has been ordered to stand trial on sexual assault charges after a hearing Tuesday that hinged on a decade-old police report in which former Temple University employee Andrea Constand told detectives that Cosby gave her three pills that made her dizzy, blurry-eyed and nauseated and left her legs feeling "like jelly." She says Cosby touched her sexually, without her consent, while her body was "frozen." Of the nearly 60 decades-old accusations of drugging and/or rape made against Cosby since 2014, only Constand's accusations have resulted in criminal charges.

    It might break your heart, but your baby is fine

    It's about to get noisy in here. A new study says that parents who let their babies cry it out or cry until they fall asleep are not causing long-term emotional or behavior harm to their tot. To test whether the infants allowed to cry it out were experiencing long-term stress, the researchers measured the stress hormone cortisol in the babies saliva in the afternoon and the morning. The findings are in line with a 2012 study of 326 children, which saw no long-term negative impacts five years later. Conclusion: Do what works best for you and your child.
